\chapter{A quantum ontology}
In this chapter, we present a complete example of rewriting, starting from an ontology expressed in OWL (Section~\ref{sec:owl}), and arriving at a Prolog formulation suitable for the pipeline presented in the previous chapter.

Following the literature, we begin with the translation of the ontology into Prolog, discussing how to translate concepts such as classes, subclasses, individuals, and relations. We then focus on the incompatibility of this first version with QA-Prolog, highlight the reasons for this incompatibility, and propose possible solutions.

Finally, we present some examples of queries to demonstrate the equivalence of the two transformations and provide observations on the final result obtained after the transformations performed by QA-Prolog.

\section{Starting ontology}
The ontology we will translate in Prolog is not the one presented in Chapter~\ref{ch:ont} but is a more complex in order to show an example more similar to a real ontology. 

The domain of interest of our ontology is the animal kingdom; this domain serves as a pretext for a deeper description than the one shown in the family tree example (Section~\ref{sec:ont_ex}). We will see how to describe animal species, their feeding habits, and the relationships between individuals.

\subsection{Ontology structure}
In this ontology, we describe a small part of the animal kingdom. The more complex class hierarchy represents the taxonomy of animals; it characterizes living beings with increasing precision until identifying their species.

The other classes instead focus feeding habits of animals. We distinguish between prey and predators, and between carnivorous and herbivorous animals (in this small example, for simplicity, we ignore omnivores\footnote{The reason will be clear in the next section.}).

Finally, we consider relationships between animals. We define two types of relationships: parental relation that connect two animals in parent–son roles, and relation related to feeding habits that link two animals in the roles of predator and prey.

The T-Box of our ontology can be seen in Figure~\ref{fig:ont_animal}, we define also some individual and their relation to populate our ontology\footnote{The code for this ontology is available at \url{URL}}.

\subsection{Ontology constraints}
In this section, we present the constraints on classes and individuals represented in our ontology. These constraints allow us to make inference on the ontology and derive new information from what is already present, also thanks to these constraints we can determine if all the information contained in the ontology is consistent with the theoretical model we are describing.

Constrains defined in animal ontology are:
\begin{itemize}
	\item Constrain on classes:
	\begin{itemize}
		\item  carnivore and herbivore are disjoint classes;
	\end{itemize}
	\item Constrain on individual:
	\begin{itemize}
		\item If animal $A$ hunt $B$, $A$ is a predator and carnivore and $B$ is a prey.
	\end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

From defined constraints we can see that a prey and predator are not disjoint classes and be a prey does not automatically mean that the animal is a herbivore. This is coherent with the animal kingdom where a certain animal could be both a prey and a predator.

It should also be clear why we have omitted the omnivores from our description, this simplification allows us to describe herbivore and carnivore as disjoint and to infer that an animal that hunt is automatically a carnivore.

\subsection{Ontology contents}
We now examine some facts represented in the ontology; these will serve as examples to show how integrity checking of the information collected in the KB and the reasoning processes work.

Let us begin by considering the classes. As shown in the T-Box of Figure~\ref{fig:ont_animal}, the animals we can describe are: sardines, penguins, seals, polar bears, and reindeer. From T-Box we also see that we have only specified reindeer are herbivorous, feeding habits of other spices can be inferred with hunt relationship.

We now examine some facts about the individuals that populate our KB:
\begin{itemize}
	\item Beary is a polar bear and hunt Sealy and Penguy;
	\item Sealy is a seal and hunt Penguy;
	\item Penguy is a penguin and hunt Sardy;
	\item Sardy is a sardine;
	\item Reiny is a reindeer and hunt Sardy;
	\item Reiny\_b  and Reiny\_c are reindeer, Reiny\_b is son of Reiny and Reiny\_c is son of Reiny\_b; 
\end{itemize}

From this information, we can deduce that Beary and Reiny are predators; Sealy and Penguy are both predators and prey; and Sardy is prey.

\paragraph{Contraddiction: } A contradiction has also been deliberately introduced into the ontology. From the fact that Reiny hunts, it follows that she is a carnivorous animal; however, her species is labeled as herbivorous. Reiny is therefore both herbivorous and carnivorous. Moreover, herbivores and carnivores are disjoint classes. This makes the KB inconsistent.

If we used a reasoner to verify the consistency of the KB, for example HermiT\cite{glimm2014hermit}, we would obtain an error message informing us that the ontology is inconsistent and that the reasoner’s results cannot therefore be considered reliable.

The reasoner is also able to provide an explanation of the inconsistency (shown in Figure~\ref{fig:protege}\footnote{The figure shows the dialog window of Protégé\cite{musen2015protege}, a widely used ontology editor}), from which it is clear that the problem is that Reiny is simultaneously herbivorous and carnivorous.

\begin{figure}[h]
	\centering
	\includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{quantum_ontology/protege}
	\caption{Error dialog in Protégé}
	\label{fig:protege}
\end{figure}

We introduced this inconsistency in order to show \textemdash{}in the following sections\textemdash{} how it is also possible in Prolog to detect this type of error and identify contradictions in the KB. If we remove the fact \say{Reiny hunt Sardy} the ontology will return consistent.

\section{Prolog version}
To translate the ontology into a Prolog KB, we follow the work presented in~\cite{samuel2008translating}. This article discusses in depth how to replicate the various constructs provided by OWL in order to obtain a logically equivalent KB in Prolog.

In this section, we will see how to translate the concepts of class and subclass, disjoint classes, and relationships between individuals; we will present rules to infer new information, as was done in the ontology. Finally, we will show how to detect errors and inconsistencies in the knowledge base.

Our goal is not to go through the entire translation of an ontology into Prolog, but rather to convince ourselves that such a translation is possible.

The original ontology includes only a small subset of the constructs available in OWL. This is because the translation into Prolog is only the first step; in the following sections, we will modify the Prolog program to make it compatible with QA-Prolog and obtain a representation suitable for a quantum annealer.

Advanced topics such the open world assumption, enumerated classes, existential quantification, cardinality rules, cyclic hierarchies, and  more are covered in~\cite{samuel2008translating}.

\section{QA-Prolog version}
The KB we have just obtained is not compatible with the QA-Prolog pipeline. As presented in Section~\ref{sec:}, QA-Prolog does not implement all the features of Prolog In particular, lists and complex terms are not supported, and rules cannot be recursive.

In this section we will see how to modify unsupported rules in order to make them compatible with the pipeline described in Chapter~\ref{ch:}.

\subsection{Lists and complex terms}
In our KB we introduced a single list, in Listing~\ref{lst:error}, exclusively to print an additional message to explain the inconsistent proposition. The list can simply be removed, or we can use an additional variable that will be bound to the message we want to print.

Complex terms are predicates that have one or more predicates as arguments. The complex term present in our KB is \mintinline{text}|logicNot(isA(I, C))| introduced in Listing~\ref{lst:disjoint} line $3$. We used this predicate to state that if an individual belongs to a class \mintinline{text}|C1| disjoint from class \mintinline{text}|C2|, then the individual cannot belong to \mintinline{text}|C2|. 

We can replace the complex term with a new predicate that represents the same concept: \mintinline{text}|isNotA(I, C)|. This new predicate forces us to modify also the rule for capturing inconsistencies in the KB. Listing~\ref{lst:error_qa} shows the final result.

	\mylisting{.9}{Prolog}{code/quantum_ontology/error_qa.pl}
	\caption{List and complex term removal}

Eliminating the predicate \mintinline{text}|logicNot(P)| where \mintinline{text}|P| is a proposition, therefore possibly a predicate, forces us to define a new predicate for each statement we want to negate and to test each \emph{predicate}-\emph{predicate\_negation} pair to check for possible inconsistencies.

In our KB, only class membership can generate inconsistency, so a single rule is sufficient. In general, other definitions of the predicate \mintinline{text}|error| may be necessary to account for all possible causes of inconsistency\footnote{However, this also gives us the possibility to define more precise error messages}.

\subsection{Unfolding}
In our KB there are two rules that are defined recursively: \mintinline{text}|subClass(C1, C2)| and \mintinline{text}|hasproperty(I1, R, I2)|. 

To eliminate recursion, we can \emph{unfold} the recursive calls. The procedure is analogous to the \emph{loop unrolling} performed by modern compilers to speed up the execution of machine code. Loop unrolling consists of transforming a loop of instructions into a repeated sequence of instructions; this increases the length of the code but reduces the number of loop condition checks.

Unfolding consists of developing the recursion by explicitly repeating the function call; in this way the recursive call is replaced by many calls to the function \mintinline{text}|A| in the function \mintinline{text}|B|.

\paragraph{SubClasses:} To eliminate recursion in the rule \mintinline{text}|subClass(C1, C2)| we can remove the rule with the recursive call, replacing it with a sufficient number of other rules to cover the entire class hierarchy; each new rule adds one call and allows moving one level higher in the hierarchy.

Our ontology has a maximum depth of $3$, therefore at most two recursive calls are needed to start from any node in the class hierarchy and reach the root. Listing~\ref{lst:subclass_qa} shows the updated version of the rule \mintinline{text}|subClass(C1, C2)| (to clarify the procedure, unfolding developed up to three calls, even though only two were necessary to cover the entire class hierarchy).

\subsection{Stopping criterion}
As previously mentioned, it is not always possible to know in advance how many recursive calls will be performed to capture all the aspects we are interested in. In general, we can distinguish two cases: T-Box and A-Box.

\paragraph{T-Box:} The T-Box describes our domain of interest and should be stable. Once a description of the world suitable for our purpose has been developed, it is rare for it to change; in our case, a modification of the T-Box could occur only if a new species were discovered, which is not very frequent.

If the rules with recursive calls involve the T-Box, it can be possible to know the maximum number of recursive calls needed to capture all relevant information and perform sufficient unfolding to allow an adequate number of function calls.

An example of this stopping criterion was shown in the rewriting of the rule \mintinline{text}|subClass(C1, C2)|. Once the class hierarchy is known, it is possible to compute the maximum depth and use it as a criterion to stop the unfolding process.

\paragraph{A-Box:} The A-Box contains the individuals populating the ontology; for this reason, the A-Box is much more subject to change than the T-Box and, as discussed when presenting the ancestor-descendant relationship, relationships between individuals can also be very complex.

In general, there is no way to establish in advance how many recursive calls are necessary to obtain all the results we expect. Moreover, each time an individual is added to the KB or its relationships are modified, the depth of the recursive calls may change.

With regard to the A-Box, the stopping criterion for unfolding must be decided in advance or estimated through heuristics where possible. It is not possible to know in advance the maximum number of recursive calls without performing reasoning, but this would be counterproductive since the objective of the translation is to perform reasoning on a quantum annealer.

\subsection{Equivalence between the KBs}
Before replacing the KB with unsupported construct and recursive rules with the modified one, we must ensure that there is equivalence between the two KBs. 

To show equivalence between recursive calls and the unfolded version, it would be possible to construct a proof based on the principle of induction, possibly with the aid of a proof assistant.

Such a constructive proof is not the objective of this work. To convince ourselves of the equivalence and to take into account also other modification, we test the behavior of queries that make use rewritten rules to ensure their correctness.

As far as possible, the unfolding process was carried out so as to make it transparent to the end user. The result was achieved with regard to queries related to the subclass hierarchy and the retrieval of an individual's ancestors.

As for error detection, however, the various rules that define the predicate \mintinline{text}|error| may have different numbers of parameters. For each number of parameters it will be necessary to test the predicate\footnote{not for each definition of the predicate; for example, if there are two definitions with $3$ parameters and one with $4$, it will be necessary to test \mintinline{text}|error| twice.}, in order to ensure that all inconsistencies are identified.

The result of the queries is as expected: we obtained all and only the results that we would also have obtained by querying the original KB. This intuitively convinces us of the equivalence of the two KBs.

\section{Risultato finale}
La KB ottenuta\footnote{Disponibile a \url{URL}.} è compatibile con la pipeline di QA-Prolog. In questa sezione percorriamo tutta la pipeline di trasformazioni fino ad ottenere l'Hamiltoniana che rappresenta la KB con annessa query e discuteremo dei riusltati ottenuti.

Formulare una query è essenziale perchè definisce gli output del nostro programma e ci permette quindi di costriure il ciruito digitale che è il primo passo della pipeline.

Per valutare il risultato della traduzione non consideriamo il risultato della valutazione delle query perchè come già mostrato nell capitolo sulla pipeline alla Sezione~\ref{} i simulatori offerti dal framework Ocean permettono di trovare la soluzione solo per istanze di problemi molto piccole. Quello che considereremo invece è proprio la dimensione del problema di minimizzazione che otteniamo al termine della pipeline, estraendo il numero di qubit che compongono il polinomio da minimizzare e cercando di dare una giustificazione delle diverse dimensioni che otterremo.

La query $5$ aggiunge a tutte le regole necessarie a trovare la soluzione alla query $4$  anche la disgiunzione tra le classi e la regola che definisce le inconsistenze. Si passa così a $3812$ variabili. La query $6$ infine fa uso delle relazioni tra individui e in particolare necessita della relazione che definisce gli antenati di cui è stato fatto unfolding nel Listato~\ref{}. Proprio per colpa dell'unfolding il numero di regole sale molto, di conseguenza cresce notevolmente anche la dimensione del problema: fino a $9519$ \emph{qubits}.

Dai risultati ottenuti è chiaro che a seconda della query otteniamo Hamiltoniane di dimensioni molto differenti, questo è vantaggioso perchè ci permette di non considerare tutta la KB per ogni query, ma di estrarre solo la parte necessaria a rispondere alla nostra particolare domanda.

Ci si può allora interrogare quale step della pipeline sia responsabile di questa estrazione. Osservando i file nella cartella di lavoro di QA-Prolog si può notare come il file in Verilog sia uguale per tutte le query, mentre il file EDIF sintetizzato da Yosys sia radicalmente diverso da una query all'altra.

L'ottimizzazione, quindi, avviene a livello di circuito logico, una volta definito l'output del nostro circuito attaverso la query Yosis (Sezione~\ref{}) è in grado di scartare tutti quei pezzi del circuito descritto in Verilog che non sono necessari a definire l'output (non sono collegati, nel senso circiutale, all'output). Questo passaggio, come evidenziato dal grafico in Figura~\ref{} è in grado di abbattere notevolemente la dimensione del problema.

\section{Conclusion}
In questo capitolo abbiamo mostrato come si può passare da un'ontologia espressa in OWL a una KB espressa in Prolog sulla quale sia possibile evidenziare inconsistenze nei fatti e fare ragionamento come sull'ontologia di partenza.

Abbiamo richiamato alcune delle limitazioni di QA-Prolog mostrando come impattavano sulla nostra traduzione, abbiamo proposto delle soluzioni che ci hanno portato a risccrivere alcune regole, ma soprattutto a  fare unfolding delle chiamate ricorsive.

Infine abbiamo mosrtato che la KB ottenuta è effettivamente compatibile con la pipeline di QA-Prolog percorrendo tutti gli step fino ad ottenere una Hamiltoniana di cui abbiamo discusso le dimensioni correlandole alla query e di coinseguenza ai fatti e alle regole necessari per rispondere alla query stessa.

Questo capitolo può essere visto ad alto livello come uno step aggiuntivo a monte della pipeline: QA-Prolog, attraverso riscritture successive in diversi formati che logicamente rappresentano sempre los stesso problema con la stessa soluzione parte da una KB in Prolog per giungere a una rappresentazione adatta ad un quantum annealer. In questo capitolo abbiamo aggiunto la riscrittura da OWL a Prolog aumentando così di uno step la catena di riscritture.
